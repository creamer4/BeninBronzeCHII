Laws Governing Antiquities Export

  Nigeria passed its first law aimed at protecting and preserving Nigerian cultural heritage in 1932. The law, know as the Antiquities Ordinance No. 17, created the National Department of Antiquities and the Antiquities Commission, as well as established museums, archaeological digs, and protection for antiquities. Ordinance No. 17 was followed by the Antiquities Regulations of 1957, which required export permits for any antiquities or materiel heritage leaving Nigeria. While the government of Nigeria and Nigerian activists were of the opinion that cultural heritage should not leave the country, there is limited evidence to suggest the effectiveness of the heritage laws in the 50s and 60s. These laws were known by researchers, historians, and other professionals visiting and working in the country, scholars note that these antiquity ordinances did not necessarily dissuade illicit exportation in many cases.

  Due to the limited success of Ordinance No. 17 and the Export Permit regulations, the Nigerian Government passed the Prohibited Transfers Decree No. 9 in 1974, and later expanded the decree in 1979. These laws expanded the protection and restrictions surrounding Nigerian antiquities to prevent the removal of material culture from the country. They also dissolved the Antiquities Commission and the Department of Antiquities, and combined them to become the National Commission for Museums and Monuments (NCMM) which is still the primary governing body for Nigerian antiquities laws.

  The creation and passing for the Prohibited Transfer Decree corresponded with the UNESCO 1970s convention, making Nigeria one of the many countries that expanded their heritage protection laws following the international treaty for the Means of Prohibiting and Preventing the Illicit Import, Export and Transfer of Ownership of Cultural Property

  Limited primary documents relating to the original 1953 Antiquities Ordinance are available online. However, language for the later Decree No. 77 in the 1979 seems to suggest that the Ordinance was the foundational language for the defining of antiquities. Decree 77 defined antiquities as
   (a) any object of archaeological interest or land in which any such object is believed to exist or was discovered, or;
   (b) any relic of early European settlement or colonization.
   (c) any work of art or craftwork including any statue, modelled clay figure, cast or wrought in metal, carving, housepost,
     ornament, utensil, weapon, armour, regalia, manuscript, or document if such work of art or craft work is of indigenous
     origin, and;
   (d) was made or fashioned before 1918 (the period of the Great Influenza epidemic), or;
   (e) is of historical, artistic or scientific interest, and is or has been used at anytime in the performance and for the purpose
     of any traditional African ceremony
