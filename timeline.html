Benin Bronzes at the Broad

Movement of Benin Bronzes from 1960 to now

1960

Michigan State University entered a contract with USAID to assist in creating the University of Nigeria. Throughout the 1950s and 1960s, facaulty and staff from MSU lived, worked, and visited the University to aid and assess the progress being made by the University

1963

John Hannah, President of Michigan State University at the time, mentioned to Joseph Druse, Head of the Humanities at U of N, and his brother Arthur Hannah that he was interested in purchses Nigerian artifacts on behalf of MSU and the MSU Museum.

August 1963

Correspondence between Joeseph Druse and George Axinn indicates that they have purchased antiquites, and were well aware that the official position of the Nigerian Government and the Antiquities Commission was for Nigerian Artifacts to remain in the country.

December 1963

In his dictated diary, George Axinn mentioned recieving bronze or wood carvings and statues from John Hannah and was asked to ship the pieces back to the US on behalf of MSU. He passed the artifacts to Bud Doane to ship the items

January 1964

John Hannah wrote Mr. May explaining that his brother purchsed pieces of Benin antiquities on behalf of himself and the University, and John would like to reimburse Arthur. John also mentioned that there may be some difficulty in exporting the pieces due to antiquities law in Nigeria.

1964

Bud Doane ran into trouble with customs when trying to import the bronzes pieces. After some back and forth, with Bud not providing the correct documents and information, the customs officials decided to allow the objects into the country, as they were being delivered to a university museum.

1964

The bronze pieces arrived at the Museum. They came in several "batches", lilely die to the chanllenges with customs officials. They were accessioned to the Kresge Art Museum Collection, which is now the MSU Broad Art Museum collection.

1964

The pieces became part of permanent display at the Kresge Art Museum

1989

One piece from the Benin collection was displayed during the exhibiton Expressions of Afirca, which was produced by curator Dr. Raymon Silverman. In his label for the piece, Dr. Silverman wrote that the piece was not as old as John Hannah was led to believe, but was rather a more contemporary peice that was doctored to look old. Dr. Silverman explained this was very common in the 60s, and cited the patina and physical features as the reason for this assessment.

2022

Several of the pieces are displayed in the exhibiton History Told Slant, curated by Steven Bridges
